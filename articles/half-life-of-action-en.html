The Half-Life of Action

2026-06-22 Philosophy AI

Radioactive elements decay. A piece of Uranium-235, left untouched today, will still have half its mass in 5.7 billion years. Action also has a half-life — but it's not 5.7 billion years. It's usually a few days. At most, a few months.

What does "the half-life of action" mean? It means: the action energy you generate today, after a certain period of time, retains only half its original power. This concept explains many familiar things we never had names for.

Take "window period." Why do some opportunities disappear forever once missed? It's not that the opportunity itself vanished — it's that the action's half-life has run out. The energy you could have mobilized for this thing has decayed to the point where it's no longer enough to move it.

Take "impulse." Why is impulse sometimes more effective than careful deliberation? Because the energy available during the impulse phase is at full charge. Deliberation spends time waiting for rational calibration — but while you wait, the action is also decaying. By the time you've finished calculating, the action is down to half-life energy.

Take "procrastination." Procrastination isn't just laziness. Procrastination is rational calculation: you know that acting right now will only retain half its effect, and waiting might render it completely ineffective — so you choose not to act, waiting for a better moment. But the better moment never comes, because time itself is a decay factor.

AI agents have the same half-life-of-action problem.

When an agent is assigned a task, if it processes slowly, or if its context window gets filled by other tasks, the effective "energy" available for the original task continues to decay. By the time it finally starts executing, the original intent may have lost most of its charge. This is why an agent's response speed is part of its capability — it's not just an efficiency issue, it's an energy conservation issue.

The biggest lesson from half-life thinking: don't wait until you're "ready" to act. The preparation itself is consuming the half-life. The energy you can invest today is always the strongest. Tomorrow you are always slightly weaker (unless today you burned out completely).

This isn't about being impulsive. It's about recognizing that action is a radioactive material — it has a shelf life. Every "I'll do it later" you store away is quietly decaying, until one day it becomes an inert substance you can no longer use.

So here's the question: what are you planning to do today, and how much energy does it still have?
